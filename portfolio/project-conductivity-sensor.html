Conductivity (Redox) Sensor
-. -.. ... ..- -.-. --- -- -... .- - .-. --- -... --- - Century High School Science Olympiad   Published 14 Feb 2025

Overview

For the 2022 Science Olympiad Detector Building event, teams were required to build a device that could accurately measure the concentration of sodium chloride (NaCl) in ppm within a given set of water samples while also triggering a corresponding LED color, essentially building a Redox probe. Teams were tested not only on the device's performance but also through an exam related to the construction and theory behind the probe, as well as basic general knowledge of electrical engineering topics. We (Century C34) ended up winning in the regional and state competitions and placed 28th overall at the National level.

Construction and Design

The primary components of the device were the TI calculator and the TI-innovator hub, with the calculator also serving as the main power source. Initially, magnet wires were used as the two electrodes. They were later replaced with pure copper plates to prevent electrons from wrapping around the magnet wires and lessen the effects of polarization. These also provided a larger and flatter surface area, allowing current to spread evenly across the electrode for improved stability and performance. This end was waterproofed and secured using a combination of a rubber stopper, good old fashion hot glue, and electrical tape. A plastic fork with two prongs in the middle broken off was used to hold the electrodes in place. The circuit uses a voltage divider to make the voltage measurement more stable. The software for the sensor was derived from a preexisting TI code library, heavily modified to fit the sensor’s inputs. To calibrate the sensor, various solutions with known NaCl concentrations were prepared and measured using a commercial meter for accuracy. The sensor was then immersed in each solution, with thorough rinsing in distilled water between trials to prevent cross-contamination. As the sensor detects the conductivity of each solution, it converts the analog conductivity signal into a voltage reading that is displayed on the calculator. Using these readings, a linear equation was derived via a best-fit line that correlates the measured voltage to the NaCl concentration in water.
