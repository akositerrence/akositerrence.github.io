BOULDERBOT

Boulderbot is a 30 lb combat robot I am leading the development of as part of NDSU's Combat Robotics team. Its namesake comes from bouldering, a specific discipline from the sport of rock climbing that much of the project members enjoy in our spare time. As such, most of the aesthetic elements on the robot are inspired by rock climbing.

PRELIMINARY DESIGN

Much of the robot's design was based on skills and experience developed with Cowbot, Boulderbot's predecessor, as four out of five members (one graduated) from that crew are returners for this project. Development began in early September with preliminary brainstorming of different ideas to rectify shortcomings from previous seasons.

Multiple issues from Cowbot needed to be addressed:

Size : Cowbot was incredibly compact, leading to difficulty accessing internals between matches.
Mobility : Cowbot can't drive upside down, and was essentially an incapacitated turtle if flipped over.
Mobility again : Cowbot slow.
Chain Drive : They suck to deal with if not done right.
Weapon : Cowbot's timing belt on the weapon was improperly tensioned.
DFM/DFA : Cowbot had inaccessible bolt holes and unnecessary tight tolerances.
Wiring : Cowbot had no space for wires.

... and many more!

In light of these issues, Boulderbot was designed to counter these deficiencies.

STRUCTURAL DESIGN

The most common failure mode for combat robots is not necessarily catastrophic structural failure, but rather a failure of interior components due to high shock loads. Therefore, much emphasis was placed on manufacturability and assemblage rather than full inherent rigidity. In contrast to Cowbot, a tab and slot method was used for major components such as the main plates to make assembly more intuitive. Boulderbot is also noticebly much larger in dimension than Cowbot to ease access to internal components and make space for auxiliary elements such as fuses and any necessary cushionining components.

The frame's profile needed to permit the robot to drive upside down incase it is knocked from its usual orientation. This is especially important for robots not symmetric about its x-axis. A common remedy is to add a smaller, free-spinning wheel mounted above the front-forward portion of the robot. Consequently, this forces the top plating of the robot to be reduced to allow clearance for the wheels to touch the ground in the inverted state.

The main material used for the robot's chassis is 6061-T6 and 5032-H32 aluminum, while the outside armor is made out of TPU, a very flexible plastic that alllows for opponents to tear out chunks and absorb high shock loads. 1045 steel was used for critical elements such as the weapon shaft and bottom plate, while HDPE was used for non-critical supporting components such as the gearbox and fuse mounts.

To validate the structural integrity of the chassis, I performed FEA through Ansys LS-DYNA to model impact events on different spatial configurations of the robot.

WEAPON DESIGN

The weapon's effectiveness is not as simple as how fast it can spin. A viable metric of weapon capability for combat robots is best measured through its 'bite' - the maximum insertion of a spinning weapon at a given RPM and forward attack speed. A weapon spinning too fast will simply 'chip' away at the opponent surface and cause superficial damage rather than 'biting' into their armor and exerting a torque on the opposing vehicle.
